Consulting engagement model

A management-consulting structure for agile engineering delivery: diagnose before design, deliver in evidence-backed increments, prove readiness, then embed ownership.

Neon AI Cloud consulting engagement flow

Six phases: Mobilise, Diagnose, Architect, Deliver in agile increments, Prove, and Embed, with feedback from Prove to Deliver and from Embed back to Diagnose for the next wave.

  1. Mobilise — align sponsors, scope, and non-negotiable constraints.
  2. Diagnose — establish workloads, gaps, risk posture, and measurable success criteria.
  3. Architect — design options and interfaces; define acceptance before build accelerates.
  4. Deliver — execute agile increments that produce artefacts and evidence each cycle.
  5. Prove — validate readiness; incomplete proof returns work to the next increment.
  6. Embed — transfer ownership, runbooks, and operating baselines; compound into the next wave.

Closed delivery loop

Each engagement moves from framing to operable ownership. Validation is not a final gate— it is continuous proof that the system meets its criteria.

01 Discover

Frame the problem

Workloads, constraints, risk posture, and success criteria are captured before design begins.

02 Design

Architect with intent

Interfaces, tenancy, operability, and failure modes are designed as first-class concerns.

03 Implement

Build to criteria

Delivery proceeds in measurable milestones with telemetry and ownership in mind.

04 Validate

Prove readiness

Behaviour, performance, resilience, and handover artefacts are validated with evidence.

05 Operate

Leave systems owned

Runbooks, baselines, and clear ownership so platforms remain durable after engagement.

Validation

What “done” must prove

  • Acceptance criteria are explicit and testable.
  • Behaviour under expected load and failure modes is evidenced.
  • Operational telemetry and ownership boundaries are in place.
  • Handover artefacts allow the receiving team to run and evolve the system.

Evidence

Artefacts we leave behind

  • Architecture and interface contracts
  • Implementation baselines and configuration records
  • Validation plans and observed results
  • Runbooks, ownership maps, and operating limits

Principles

How decisions are made

  • Every artefact should have a communicative or operational purpose.
  • Simplicity is deliberate reduction, not missing work.
  • Validation is part of delivery, not an optional afterthought.
  • Systems should be operable by the teams that inherit them.
